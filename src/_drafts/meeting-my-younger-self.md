I've been building a [scrapbook of social media][scrapbook], a place where I can save posts and conversations that I want to remember.
It has a nice web-based interface for browsing, and a carefully-designed data model that should scale as I add more platforms and more years of my life.

But what about everything I'd saved before?

Across various disks, I'd accumulated over 150,000 posts from Twitter, Tumblr, and other platforms -- snapshots preserved with more enthusiasm than organisation.
These sites were so important to me that I kept trying to design better backups, hoping not to lost those memories.
The archives were chaotic, devoid of context, and impossible to search -- but they were *there*.

After so many aborted attempts, my scrapbook finally feels sustainable.
I wanted to bring my older fragments into it, but importing everything wholesale would just reproduce the same problem.
I'd be polluting a clean space with a decade of disordered history.
So instead of a bulk import, I chose to curate.

I went through each post, one-by-one, and asked: *is this worth keeping?*
*Do I want this in the story of my life, or should I let it fade?*

That's how I started looking back over fifteen years of a life lived online, which became an unexpectedly emotional meeting with my younger self.

[scrapbook]: /2025/social-media-scrapbook/

## The Internet as a teacher

One thing I'd forgotten is how much I learnt from being online, especially in fannish spaces.
My timeline taught me about feminism and consent; about disability and the barriers of the built world; about racism in a way that went far deeper than anything I encountered in school.
I could learn about issues directly from the people who faced them, not filtered through a journalist's lens.

It was a crash course in humanity -- broader, richer, and more diverse than anything I got from formal education.

Once I learned to shut up and just listen, Twitter let me follow conversations between people whose lives were nothing like mine.
I got the answers to so many questions I'd never even known to ask, and I miss that.
I stopped using Twitter after it was bought by Elon Musk, and I have yet to find another platform that replicates that passive, ambient learning.

## Questioning and queer

More than anything else I saw online, queer culture has shaped my life.
I'm queer, my partner is queer, and so are most of my friends.
There are so many people I'd never have met if social media hadn't shown me this world.

When I was realising I was queer, it all felt very difficult and angsty.
Looking back, I can see myself following a clichéd path -- talking to queer people, being a loud and enthusiastic ally, then starting to realise there might be a reason I cared so much.
I went through it once when I realised I wasn't straight, and again a few years later when I realised I wasn't cis.

My younger self was oblivious, but it's all so obvious in hindsight.
I cringe at some of those older posts, but they helped me become who I am today, and I want to keep them.

## I was annoying and rude, but I grew up

There are other posts I look back with less fondness.
I'm embarrassed by how annoying I was when I was younger.
I spent too much time on self-indulgent moralising and pointless arguments, often with people I probably agreed with on almost everything else.
I wanted to be right more than I wanted to listen, and it wasn't useful to anyone.

Those arguments were worthless then and worthless now.
Deleting them was a relief.

Despite ruffling a lot of feathers, I was only the ["main character"][main-character] of Twitter once, and in a niche corner of tech.
It was an unpleasant weekend, and I got off lightly compared to some of my friends.
I've never forgotten how quickly online attention can turn to anger and hostility.

Learning about [parasocial relationships][parasocial] helped me change how I talked to people online.
I shifted my attention towards friends rather than strangers, and when I did talk to people who didn't know me, I tried to be constructive instead of showing off.
(And of course, I learned to read the replies before posting my own.)

In the end, I came to value kindness and empathy far more than cleverness and nitpicking.
When I joined Twitter, I admired by people who were smart.
Today, I look up to people who are kind.

## The ghosts in the posts

Looking through old conversations, I see the ghosts of old friendships and relationships I've since lost.
Some of those could be recovered if either of us reached out; others are gone for good.
A few people have even passed away.
I don't know where most of my old friends ended up, but I hope life has been kind to them.

There are so many spaces I passed through: the PyCon UK community; fandoms like the Marmfish, the Creampuffs, and the *Lizzie Bennet Diaries* crowd; the trans elders who supported me during my transition; the small, loyal group of readers who always left thoughtful comments.
Some I lost touch with while I was still on Twitter; others I left behind when I left Twitter altogether.

I saved my mentions as well as my own tweets, and some of them still catch me off guard.
One made me cry: *"One of the things I miss the most from my pre-pandemic Twitter timeline is seeing @alexwlchan traveling on trains and taking train selfies 💜"*.
I miss that culture too -- especially in queer and trans spaces, where selfies were such a source of joy and affirmation.
I miss seeing pretty pictures of my friends, and sharing mine in return.

I was reminded me of [a quote by C.S. Lewis][inner-ring]: *"I must not assume that you have ever first neglected, and finally shaken off, friends whom you really loved and who might have lasted you a lifetime, in order to court the friendship of those who appeared to you more important, more esoteric."*
I definitely neglected friendships through social media, and my life is poorer for it.
I'm trying to do better.

Elon Musk's purchase of Twitter shattered a lot of those connections.
Some people moved to other platforms; others stepped away from social media entirely.
I was one of them.
I still write here, but it's a much more professional space, and there's less of the personal chatter that made Twitter feel like a community.
I miss the friendships I had, and the ones that might have been.

[inner-ring]: https://www.lewissociety.org/innerring/

## What I choose to remember

I started with 150,000 fragments, and I've boiled them down to 4,000 conversations I thought worth keeping.
A lot of it I was glad to forget, but there are gems I want to remember.
Moments of kindness and insight and growth.
I'm glad I've done this, and it validates my belief that social media is an important part of my life that I should preserve properly.

Curating these memories has made them feel smaller and more manageable.
The mess of JSON files scattered across disks has been replaced by a meaningful, well-organised collection I can look back on with a smile.

## What comes next?

My use of Tumblr fell away gradually, and I stopped tweeting when Elon Musk bought Twitter.
Today my social media is mostly just cross-posting links to this site.
I wanted to pause and reflect on my relationship with social media before jumping to another platform.

Those sites filled a specific gap, and a lot of good things in my life today started as conversations on those platforms.
I do wonder how much I've missed out on.

It wasn't all good.
There were definitely times when my relationship with social media was unhealthy – "addiction" might be too strong a word, but I can see traces of it.
I spent more time there than I should, and since I stopped, that energy has gone elsewhere.

I don’t know what my future with social media will look like.
I've learnt what I miss, and what's best left in the past.
I'd like to think I've grown up a bit, that I'd be a better friend if I started using it again.
I don't know if I'll return, or in what form, but this project has me wondering.

Whatever I do next, my scrapbook lets me see the best of what's already been -- the friendships, the joy, and the moments that mattered.
